The original Capital Property Group site loaded in 6.8 seconds on mobile. The contact form was broken. Half the property listings pointed to 404 pages. The dark theme they wanted was implemented as black text on a dark gray background — literally unreadable on some monitors.

They came to us not because they wanted a website redesign. They came because their website was actively losing them deals. A property investor had emailed them a screenshot of the broken contact form with the message: "Tried to reach you about the Joplin fourplex. Guess you're not interested."

That's when they called us.

## What we inherited

Capital Property Group operates four distinct business lines out of Southwest Missouri: residential sales, rental property management, storage unit facilities, and investor services. The original site tried to serve all four audiences with a single homepage and a navigation menu that looked like a phone book.

The template they'd bought three years earlier was a WordPress theme marketed to real estate agents. It assumed one business model: agent lists houses, buyers find agent, agent sells houses. Capital's actual business was nothing like that. They needed:

- Buyers browsing MLS listings with saved searches
- Renters checking availability and submitting applications
- Storage customers seeing unit sizes and reserving online
- Investors reviewing portfolio performance and deal flow

The template handled exactly one of those use cases, poorly. The other three were bolted on through plugins that fought each other for database resources and broke every time WordPress auto-updated.

When we ran a Lighthouse audit, the performance score was 23. Not a typo. The largest contentful paint was over 8 seconds. The cumulative layout shift was catastrophic — the page literally jumped around as seventeen different tracking scripts loaded asynchronously.

## The business problem underneath the technical mess

Here's what the broken real estate website was actually costing them.

Their property management business had a 34% lead-to-tour conversion rate before we started. Industry average for their market is around 45%. They were losing eleven points of conversion somewhere, and exit analytics showed most bounces happened within the first four seconds — before the page even finished loading.

The storage business was worse. They had 340 units across two facilities. Occupancy was running at 71% while competitors with functional websites were at 88%+. The difference was that competitors let you reserve a unit online. Capital's site had a "Call for availability" button that went to a Google Voice number someone checked twice a week.

The investor services arm — their highest-margin business — had no web presence at all. It was a line item in the navigation that went to a page saying "Coming Soon" for nineteen months.

## Why we didn't just fix the WordPress site

The client's first question was reasonable: "Can you just clean up what we have?"

We spent four hours auditing the existing setup. The WordPress install had 23 active plugins, 14 of which hadn't been updated in over two years. The theme itself was abandoned — the developer's website returned a 502. The hosting was shared, with no CDN, no caching layer, and a PHP version from 2019.

We could have fixed it. We estimated twelve weeks to untangle the plugin conflicts, migrate to modern hosting, rebuild the broken templates, and add the missing functionality. The result would be a WordPress site that worked, loaded in maybe three seconds, and would need ongoing maintenance every time a plugin updated.

Or we could rebuild from scratch in Next.js in ten weeks and hand them something that would run itself for years.

The math wasn't close. We rebuilt.

## Design decisions that actually mattered

Capital wanted a dark theme. Their brand colors were black and gold, and they'd seen competitor sites with that premium dark aesthetic. The original template had attempted this and failed because dark themes are genuinely hard to execute.

The problem with most dark real estate websites is contrast. Property photos are bright. White walls, sunny windows, green lawns. Drop those into a dark UI and they look washed out or garish. The interface competes with the content.

We solved this with what we called "controlled brightness zones." The chrome — navigation, sidebars, footers — stays dark. But property cards and listing detail pages get a subtle light background that lets photos breathe. The transition between zones uses the gold accent color as a visual bridge. Here's the Tailwind config that made it work:

```typescript
const colors = {
  background: {
    DEFAULT: "#0a0a0a",
    elevated: "#141414",
    card: "#1a1a1a",
    content: "#fafafa", // Light zone for property content
  },
  gold: {
    DEFAULT: "#d4af37",
    muted: "#b8962e",
    bright: "#f4cf47",
  },
  text: {
    primary: "#ffffff",
    secondary: "#a3a3a3",
    inverted: "#0a0a0a", // For light zones
  },
};
```

Every component knows which zone it lives in. A `PropertyCard` in a grid uses dark styling. The same `PropertyCard` on a listing detail page uses light styling. One component, context-aware rendering.

The other major design decision was information architecture. Four business lines, four distinct user journeys, one homepage. We stopped trying to serve everyone at once.

The homepage now has four clear entry points — Buy, Rent, Storage, Invest — each with a single sentence and a single button. No paragraph explanations. No feature lists. Just "Buying a home in SW Missouri? Start here." Click, and you're in a flow designed entirely for buyers. The rental flow never shows you sales listings. The investor portal never mentions storage units.

This sounds obvious. It's not how most property management websites work. Most try to cross-sell everything to everyone, and the result is that nobody can find what they came for.

## The technical rebuild

We shipped the new site on Next.js 14 (this was before 15 dropped) with the App Router, Tailwind CSS, and static generation for everything that could be static.

The MLS integration was the interesting technical challenge. Capital uses a regional MLS that provides data via RETS, a protocol from 2002 that makes SOAP look modern. The original WordPress site had a plugin that synced MLS data every six hours, stored it in the WordPress database, and served it through PHP templates. Every listing page was a database query.

We inverted the model. A background job pulls MLS data every fifteen minutes, transforms it into a normalized schema, and writes it to a PostgreSQL database we control. The Next.js site reads from that database at build time for the main listing grids and uses ISR (Incremental Static Regeneration) with a five-minute revalidation window for individual listing pages.

The result: listing pages that load in under 400ms instead of 3+ seconds, with data that's never more than twenty minutes stale. For a real estate website, twenty minutes is plenty fresh.

```typescript
// Simplified listing page with ISR
export async function generateStaticParams() {
  const listings = await db.query.listings.findMany({
    where: eq(listings.status, "active"),
    columns: { mlsId: true },
  });
  return listings.map((l) => ({ mlsId: l.mlsId }));
}

export const revalidate = 300; // 5 minutes

export default async function ListingPage({
  params,
}: {
  params: { mlsId: string };
}) {
  const listing = await db.query.listings.findFirst({
    where: eq(listings.mlsId, params.mlsId),
  });

  if (!listing) notFound();

  return <ListingDetail listing={listing} />;
}
```

For storage, we built a real-time availability widget. This was the one place we needed actual interactivity. Unit availability changes throughout the day as people reserve and move out. We used a simple polling approach — the widget fetches availability every thirty seconds when visible, using `requestIdleCallback` to avoid blocking the main thread. No WebSockets, no complexity. Storage customers don't need millisecond-accurate data; they need data that's accurate within a minute.

The investor portal was the most straightforward piece, ironically. It's a gated section with Clerk authentication, showing portfolio dashboards, historical returns, and current deal flow. The data comes from Capital's internal systems via a simple REST API we helped them spec out. Nothing fancy — just clean data visualization with Recharts and a document library for K-1s and operating agreements.

## Structured data and local SEO

A real estate website lives or dies by local search. "Homes for sale in Joplin MO" needs to find you, not Zillow.

We implemented comprehensive Schema.org markup for every content type:

- `RealEstateAgent` for the company
- `RealEstateListing` for every property
- `LocalBusiness` with `areaServed` for each county they operate in
- `SelfStorage` for the storage facilities
- `FAQPage` for the common questions sections

The structured data isn't just for Google. It's for rich snippets, for voice search, for AI assistants that are increasingly how people find local services. When someone asks Siri "storage units near me in Carthage," the structured data is what makes Capital show up.

We also built programmatic landing pages for every city and service combination. "Property management in Webb City" gets its own page with localized content, testimonials from Webb City clients, and listings filtered to that area. These pages are generated at build time from a simple config:

```typescript
const serviceAreas = [
  { city: "Joplin", county: "Jasper" },
  { city: "Webb City", county: "Jasper" },
  { city: "Carthage", county: "Jasper" },
  { city: "Carl Junction", county: "Jasper" },
  // ... 12 more
];

const services = ["property-management", "homes-for-sale", "rentals", "storage"];

// Generates 64 landing pages at build time
export async function generateStaticParams() {
  return serviceAreas.flatMap((area) =>
    services.map((service) => ({
      city: slugify(area.city),
      service,
    })),
  );
}
```

Sixty-four pages, each targeting a specific long-tail keyword, all generated from templates with localized content. The SEO impact was immediate.

## The numbers after launch

We launched the redesigned site in February 2026. Here's what changed in the first ninety days:

**Performance:** Lighthouse score went from 23 to 94. Largest contentful paint dropped from 8.2 seconds to 1.1 seconds. Time to interactive dropped from 11 seconds to 1.8 seconds.

**Rental leads:** Lead-to-tour conversion went from 34% to 51%. That's seventeen points of recovery, mostly from people who previously bounced before the page loaded.

**Storage occupancy:** Went from 71% to 84% in ninety days. The online reservation system processed 47 reservations in the first month — customers who previously would have called, gotten voicemail, and gone to a competitor.

**Organic search:** Impressions for "property management" keywords in their service area increased 340%. Click-through rate improved from 1.2% to 3.8% — partly the structured data, partly the improved meta descriptions we wrote for every page.

**Investor inquiries:** This one surprised everyone. The investor portal, which had been "Coming Soon" for nineteen months, generated eight qualified investor inquiries in the first sixty days. Two of those turned into funded deals. The portal didn't do anything magical — it just existed and looked professional.

## What we'd do differently

Two things.

First, we'd push harder on the MLS data pipeline from day one. We initially tried to reuse some of the WordPress plugin's data transformation logic, thinking it would save time. It didn't. The data was malformed in ways that took weeks to debug. Should have started fresh.

Second, we'd build the investor portal as a separate subdomain with its own deployment. It's currently a route group in the main site, which means investor portal deploys require rebuilding the entire marketing site. For a site with 200+ listing pages, that's a fifteen-minute build. Not a crisis, but annoying.

## The lesson

The Capital Property Group project reinforced something we already believed: a website redesign is never really about the website. It's about the business problems the broken website is causing.

Capital didn't need a prettier site. They needed a site that loaded fast enough for impatient renters, that let storage customers reserve without playing phone tag, that made investors take them seriously, and that showed up in local search when buyers went looking.

The dark theme and the gold accents and the smooth animations — those are nice. But they're not why occupancy went from 71% to 84%. That happened because the "Reserve Now" button finally worked.
